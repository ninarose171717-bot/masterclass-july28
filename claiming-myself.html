Where we go together

Four months. Four phases.

Phase one

Noticing yourself

The clothes in your closet you never let yourself wear. The relationship you call fine. The way you answer I'm good before you've even checked in with yourself. We find the places you left, and when, and what leaving kept you safe from. Just the strange relief of being seen, finally, even by your own eyes.

Phase two

Staying with yourself

Your whole life you were taught to get away from a hard feeling as fast as possible. Fix it, numb it, breathe it out, scroll past it. Here you learn to stay. To feel the fear, the wanting, the anger, the grief, the pleasure, and not leave your own body to escape it.

When you can stay with yourself through the hard moment, everything you want stops being dangerous to receive.

Phase three

Expressing yourself

Now it comes out of you. You say the true thing while your voice is still shaking. You want out loud. You let someone be disappointed and you don't collapse or chase them down to smooth it over.

Phase four

Including yourself

Not a project you're working on. A woman who is finally in the room for her own life. You include yourself while you're dating, working, resting, arguing, celebrating, falling apart. You feel your life from the inside of it.

My story

I built this out of my own life.

It was a Friday afternoon, and from the outside I had it all handled. The nine to five, the constant dating, the strong woman who didn't need anyone's help.

Up close it was different. Empty wine bottles in the bin to get through the nights. A restless anxiety in my chest that never switched off. I was an empty shell, performing my life instead of living it. I was the emotional glue for everyone else, and I was completely unexpressed, like I was shouting underwater and no one could hear me.

Then my mum died. A sudden accident. The main pillar of my whole world, gone.

I stood in my apartment afterward and it hit me like a punch in the gut. Without a man to claim me, without my mother to approve of me, I had no idea who I was. And if I died the next day, I couldn't honestly say I had ever lived.

Something desperate woke up in me. I wanted to feel full on the inside. An abundance that didn't depend on who was loving me or what my bank account said.

And the fear came straight after.

I felt stupid for wanting more.

I told myself to just be normal, to push it down.

I was so sure I was being too much.

I was terrified that if I started choosing for myself, I'd get it horribly wrong and my whole life would fall apart.

But my mum's life ended in a heartbeat, and I couldn't unknow that. Life is too fast to spend it joyless. I moved to the other side of the world, supposedly for six months. I'm still here.

A mentor taught me the language of the nervous system, and I threw myself into the healing world. Every course, every modality, thousands spent on shiny promises. I became a self-help junkie. I could explain in perfect detail why I was the way I was, and I was still standing in the pantry at midnight, lonely and exhausted.

Then it hit me. As long as I was trying to fix myself, I was just leaving myself all over again.

So I stopped trying to fix, and I started staying. I stopped waiting to be claimed and started accompanying myself. I stopped controlling my life and started feeling my own body. I stayed through the discomfort instead of running from it, and I said true things without a twenty minute apology first.

Today I have the audacity to only do what makes me feel alive. I live on purpose. My choices are mine, and I stand by my own side when I make them. I quit the busy-ness Olympics. The fear still shows up, I'm human, but it's bearable now, because this version of me finally has my own back.

I stopped being useful to the world and became the owner of my life. That is what I want for you.

Questions you might be asking

How is this different from everything else I've done?

Most of what you've done has worked on one layer at a time. The insight, or the practice, or the identity work. What we're doing here runs all of it together: the somatic work, the parts work, the identity work, all at the same time, in the same room. That's why the things you understood long ago finally get to land in your body.

I'm already in therapy. Can I do both?

Yes, and I'd encourage it. This work isn't therapy and it doesn't replace it. It runs alongside good therapeutic support, because we're working at a different layer: the body, the breath, the parts of you that talk therapy doesn't always reach. Women often find this work helps the therapy go deeper.

How much time do I need each week?

One session per week, up to 75 minutes: sixty minutes together, with a fifteen-minute buffer so we never have to rush what is just beginning to open. Plus a weekly ten-minute practice I record specifically for you. That practice isn't optional. It's the thread that carries the work between our sessions.

Is one session a week really enough?

The session isn't where the change happens. It's where we look at what happened, and where I give you something to carry into the week. The change happens on the Tuesday you notice yourself leaving, and come back. That's why the weekly practice isn't optional.

What if I don't feel anything in my body?

Then we start there, gently. Numbness isn't a failure of the work. It's something that once kept you safe, and we don't force our way past it. You don't need to be good at feeling to begin. And if you cry, or go blank, or laugh at the wrong moment, none of that is a problem here.

What if it doesn't feel like the right fit?

You have two full weeks. If after our first sessions your body tells you this isn't right for you, tell me and I'll refund you completely. No awkwardness. I only want you here if you truly want to be. After those two weeks, we are both all the way in.

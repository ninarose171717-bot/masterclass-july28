The journey

Four months. Four movements.

Month one

Meeting yourself

Not fixing yourself. Not healing yourself. Meeting yourself. We look at who you are and what your life has quietly been organized around, with honesty, and with kindness for how it got this way.

Month two

Staying with yourself

The heart of the work. Learning to feel what’s alive in you, your needs, fear, desire, tenderness, anger, grief, pleasure, without leaving.

Stress says: I’ve lost myself. Pressure says: I’m still here. This month builds the capacity to stay.

Month three

Standing by yourself

The relationship becomes visible. Boundaries, honesty, desire. Saying what’s true instead of going quiet and inward. Receiving a no without making it mean something about your worth.

An open heart, with discernment about who has access to it.

Month four

Living as yourself

Not integration. Participation. Including yourself while drinking tea, deciding, dating, working, resting, arguing, grieving. You stop watching your life from outside and start living it from within.

My story

I know this terrain, because I lived it.

It was a Friday afternoon, and from the outside, I had it all handled. The nine-to-five. The constant dating. The strong woman who didn’t need anyone’s help.

If you had looked closer, you would have found the wine bottles I used to quiet my nights, and a restless, vibrating anxiety living in my chest. I was performing my life instead of living it. I was the emotional glue for everyone around me, and I felt completely unexpressed. Like shouting underwater.

Then my mother died. A sudden accident. The main pillar of my world, gone in an instant.

I remember standing in the quiet of my home, understanding for the first time that without a partner to claim me and without a mother to approve of me, I didn’t actually know who I was. And that if I died tomorrow, I couldn’t honestly say I had lived.

A new, desperate desire was born that day: I wanted to feel full inside. A richness that didn’t depend on who loved me or what my bank account looked like.

The fear arrived immediately. I felt stupid for wanting more. Too much. Certain that if I started making decisions for myself, I’d make some catastrophic mistake and my whole life would collapse.

But my mother’s life was gone in a heartbeat, and that became my catalyst: life is too fast to spend it joyless. I moved countries, for six months. I never moved back.

A mentor introduced me to the language of the nervous system, and I threw myself into the healing world. I tried everything the industry told me to try. I spent thousands. I became a self-help junkie with all the intellectual knowledge in the world, and I was still standing in the pantry at midnight, lonely and exhausted.

Until it landed: as long as I was trying to fix myself, I was abandoning myself. Again.

So I stopped seeking to be claimed, and I began accompanying myself. I stopped trying to control my life and started noticing my body. I stayed with myself through discomfort instead of running back to comfort. I began saying true things without a twenty-minute internal apology first.

Today, I have the audacity to only do things that make me feel alive. My decisions are mine. Good or bad doesn’t matter; I stand by my own side when I make them. Fear and doubt still visit, because I’m human. But they’re tolerable now, because this version of me finally has my own back.

I’m no longer a utility for the world. I’m the owner of my life.

Questions you might be asking

How is this different from everything else I’ve done?

Most of what you’ve done has worked on one layer at a time. The insight, or the practice, or the identity work. What we’re doing here runs all of it together: the somatic work, the parts work, the identity work, at the same time, inside the same container. That’s why things that were understood before finally get to land.

I’m already in therapy. Can I do both?

Yes, and I’d encourage it. This work isn’t therapy and it doesn’t replace it. It runs alongside good therapeutic support, because we’re working at a different layer: the body, the breath, the parts of you that talk therapy doesn’t always reach. Women often find this work helps the therapy go deeper.

How much time do I need each week?

One session per week, up to 75 minutes: 60 minutes together, with a 15-minute buffer so we never have to rush what’s tender. Plus a weekly 10-minute practice I record specifically for you. That practice isn’t optional. It’s the thread that carries the work between our sessions. Outside of that, this isn’t homework-heavy. It’s presence-heavy. You bring yourself. I’ll take care of the rest.

What if I fall behind or miss a session?

Life happens. We’ll handle it. Sessions are one-to-one, so if something comes up, we’ll find another time. What I’ll ask of you is honesty, with me and with yourself, about what’s actually getting in the way. Sometimes what looks like a schedule conflict is the first thing we work on together.

What if it doesn’t feel like the right fit?

You’ll know within the first week. If after our first session you feel in your body that this isn’t the right container for you right now, I’ll refund you in full. No awkwardness, no convincing. I only want you here if you’re genuinely in. After that first week, you’re committed. And so am I, completely.
